# Paper 2: Structural Differentiation and Symmetry Breaking in Network Potential Optimisation

## 

## Abstract

The Network Potential Functional (NPF) introduced in Mack (2026) defines a variational framework for relational optimisation on weighted graphs, integrating cohesion, brokerage, and redundancy into a single scalar objective. While the foundational theory established existence, optimality conditions, curvature regimes, and local stability, the structural consequences of equilibrium were not fully developed.

This paper derives the architectural implications of Network Potential optimisation. We prove that strict local maximisers generically partition incident ties into differentiated structural roles determined by brokerage and redundancy exposure. Furthermore, we demonstrate that topological perturbations—specifically the introduction of high-brokerage edges—induce symmetry-breaking transitions that increase local weight dispersion and produce persistent role-differentiated configurations. These results establish Network Potential optimisation as a structural differentiation mechanism rather than merely a static maximisation procedure.

The paper thereby transforms the equilibrium conditions of Mack (2026) into a theory of architectural emergence in weighted networks.

# 1.0 Foundational Theorem Schema

## 1.0.1 Configuration Space

Let $`G = (V,E)`$be a fixed finite graph with edge set $`E`$. A weighted network on $`G`$is specified by a vector of edge weights

``` math
w = (w_{e})_{e \in E} \in \lbrack 0,1\rbrack^{\mid E \mid}.
```

The configuration space of admissible weight assignments is therefore the compact hypercube

``` math
W = \lbrack 0,1\rbrack^{\mid E \mid},
```

with interior

``` math
W^{\circ} = (0,1)^{\mid E \mid}.
```

Points $`w \in W`$represent relational configurations on the fixed topology $`G`$. Throughout this paper the topology $`G`$is assumed fixed unless explicitly stated otherwise.

Relational optimisation is formulated through scalar functionals

``` math
\Phi:W \rightarrow \mathbb{R}
```

referred to as **relational potentials**. Equilibrium configurations correspond to stationary points of $`\Phi`$on $`W`$, with interior equilibria satisfying

``` math
\nabla\Phi(w^{*}) = 0,
```

and boundary equilibria characterised by constrained optimality conditions.

For structural analysis we restrict attention to sufficiently regular potentials. Unless otherwise stated, relational potentials are assumed to satisfy

``` math
\Phi \in C^{3}(W^{\circ}) \cap C^{0}(W).
```

Interior analysis is conducted on $`W^{\circ}`$, where differential operators are well-defined, while existence results rely on compactness of $`W`$.

The Network Potential Functional introduced in Mack (2026) provides a canonical example of a relational potential defined on $`W`$.

## 

## 1.0.2 Equilibrium Operators

Let $`\Phi:W \rightarrow \mathbb{R}`$be a relational potential satisfying the regularity conditions of Section 1.0.1. An equilibrium configuration $`w^{*} \in W^{\circ}`$is characterised by the first-order optimality condition

``` math
\nabla\Phi(w^{*}) = 0.
```

Local structural properties of equilibrium configurations are determined by the second derivative operator

``` math
H(w^{*}) = D^{2}\Phi(w^{*}),
```

referred to as the **Hessian operator** of the relational potential. This operator acts on perturbations $`\delta w \in \mathbb{R}^{\mid E \mid}`$through the quadratic form

``` math
\delta w^{T}H(w^{*})\delta w.
```

Strict local maximality requires that $`H(w^{*})`$be negative definite.

**Incident Hessian Operator**

For each node $`i \in V`$, let

``` math
\delta(i) = \{ e \in E:e\text{ is incident to }i\}
```

denote the set of edges incident to node $`i`$.

The **incident Hessian operator** at node $`i`$is defined as the restriction

``` math
H_{i}(w^{*}) = D^{2}\Phi(w^{*}) \mid_{\mathbb{R}^{\delta(i)}}.
```

Equivalently, $`H_{i}(w^{*})`$is the principal submatrix of the full Hessian corresponding to edges in $`\delta(i)`$.

The incident Hessian operator determines local curvature of the relational potential in the space of incident tie perturbations.

**Linear Response Operator**

If $`H(w^{*})`$is nonsingular, the Implicit Function Theorem implies that equilibrium weights depend differentiably on perturbation parameters.

Let $`\Phi_{\lambda}`$be a smooth parameterised family of relational potentials. Then equilibrium weights satisfy

``` math
\nabla\Phi_{\lambda}(w^{*}(\lambda)) = 0.
```

Differentiation yields the equilibrium response equation

``` math
\frac{dw^{*}}{d\lambda} = - H(w^{*})^{- 1}\frac{\partial}{\partial\lambda}\nabla\Phi_{\lambda}(w^{*}).
```

Thus, the inverse Hessian

``` math
H(w^{*})^{- 1}
```

acts as an equilibrium response operator, determining first-order variation of equilibrium weights under perturbation.

This inverse operator will be referred to as the **susceptibility kernel** of the equilibrium (Definition 3.2). It governs the multivariate comparative statics of equilibrium configurations and determines the first-order propagation of perturbations through the weighted network.

## 

## 1.0.3 Structural Index

Structural differentiation at equilibrium is characterised through the sensitivity of equilibrium weights to perturbations of the relational potential.

Let

``` math
w^{*} \in W^{\circ}
```

be an equilibrium configuration with nonsingular Hessian operator

``` math
H(w^{*}) = D^{2}\Phi(w^{*}).
```

Let

``` math
K = H(w^{*})^{- 1}
```

denote the susceptibility kernel (Definition 3.2).

Consider a smooth perturbation of the relational potential

``` math
\Phi_{\lambda} = \Phi + \lambda\Psi,
```

where

``` math
\Psi:W \rightarrow \mathbb{R}
```

is a sufficiently smooth perturbation functional and $`\lambda`$is a scalar parameter.

If $`H(w^{*})`$ is nonsingular, the Implicit Function Theorem ensures that the equilibrium weights vary differentiably with respect to $`\lambda`$. Differentiating the equilibrium condition yields

``` math
\frac{dw^{*}}{d\lambda} = - K\nabla\Psi(w^{*}).
```

This vector represents the first-order equilibrium response to perturbation.

**Definition 1.0.1 (Structural Index)**

Let

``` math
g = \nabla\Psi(w^{*})
```

denote a perturbation gradient.

The **structural index vector** is defined by

``` math
\sigma = Kg.
```

The **structural index of edge** $`e \in E`$ is

``` math
\sigma_{e} = (Kg)_{e}.
```

The structural index measures the first-order equilibrium responsiveness of edge $`e`$under perturbation.

**Incident Structural Differentiation**

For a node $`i \in V`$, structural differentiation occurs when

``` math
\sigma_{e} \neq \sigma_{f}
```

for distinct edges

``` math
e,f \in \delta(i).
```

This condition expresses differentiation in terms of equilibrium response rather than equilibrium weight magnitude.

## 1.0.4 Structural Differentiation Theorem

Structural differentiation at equilibrium arises as a generic consequence of variational optimisation under admissible relational potentials.

Let $`G = (V,E)`$be a fixed finite graph and let $`\Phi:W \rightarrow \mathbb{R}`$be a relational potential satisfying the regularity conditions of Section 1.0.1. Let $`w^{*} \in W^{\circ}`$be a strict local maximiser of $`\Phi`$.

**Theorem 1.0.1 (Structural Differentiation)**

There exists an open dense class of relational potentials such that every strict interior local maximiser $`w^{*}`$satisfies the following properties:

**(i) Hyperbolicity**

The Hessian operator

``` math
H(w^{*}) = D^{2}\Phi(w^{*})
```

is nonsingular.

**(ii) Incident Spectral Simplicity**

For each node $`i \in V`$, the incident Hessian operator

``` math
H_{i}(w^{*})
```

has simple spectrum.

**(iii) Response Differentiation**

For every node $`i \in V`$and every pair of distinct incident edges

``` math
e,f \in \delta(i),
```

the structural indices satisfy

``` math
\sigma_{e} \neq \sigma_{f}.
```

**Structural Interpretation**

The theorem establishes that differentiated equilibrium architectures arise generically from the curvature structure of admissible relational potentials. Non-differentiated configurations require degeneracy of the associated equilibrium operators and therefore occur only on exceptional subsets of the admissible class.

Structural differentiation is determined by anisotropy of the equilibrium response operator

``` math
H(w^{*})^{- 1}.
```

**Consequences**

Under the hypotheses of the theorem:

- Equilibria are locally isolated.

- Incident ties exhibit distinct equilibrium responses.

- Structural roles correspond to differentiated response directions.

Equilibrium architecture is therefore determined by the operator geometry of the relational potential.

## 1.0.5 Genericity Convention

Genericity statements in this paper refer to properties of relational potentials within the regularity class introduced in Section 1.0.1.

Let

``` math
\mathcal{A}^{3}(G) \subset C^{3}(W^{\circ}) \cap C^{0}(W)
```

denote the class of relational potentials defined on the configuration space $`W`$. This space is equipped with the standard $`C^{3}`$topology on compact subsets of $`W^{\circ}`$.

A property of relational potentials is said to hold **generically** if it holds on an open dense subset of $`\mathcal{A}^{3}(G)`$.

In particular, generic properties include:

- nonsingularity of equilibrium Hessian operators,

- simplicity of incident spectra,

- and differentiation of structural indices.

Degenerate equilibria correspond to potentials lying in lower-dimensional subsets of $`\mathcal{A}^{3}(G)`$ and therefore arise only under exceptional parameter choices.

All genericity statements in this paper are to be interpreted in this sense unless otherwise stated.

# 1.1 Structural Differentiation and Symmetry Breaking

## 1.1.1 Background

The Network Potential Functional (NPF) (Mack, 2026) defines a scalar relational potential on the space of weighted graphs $`G_{w}`$, where $`G = (V,E)`$is a finite graph and $`w \in \lbrack 0,1\rbrack^{E}`$denotes tie strengths. Within the admissible class introduced in Section 1.0, the functional integrates three structural principles:

- cohesion (stability),

- brokerage (mobility),

- redundancy (closure penalty).

Under mild regularity conditions, maximisers exist and satisfy first-order optimality conditions expressible as balance equations between these competing forces. Curvature analysis distinguishes concave regimes (unique global maxima) from non-concave regimes admitting multiple local maxima. Strict local maxima are locally stable under gradient ascent dynamics (Mack, 2026).

The foundational theory thus established that the Network Potential Functional is:

- well-defined,

- optimisable,

- and dynamically interpretable as a potential game (Monderer and Shapley, 1996).

However, the structural geometry implied by these equilibrium conditions was not fully articulated within the operator framework introduced in Section 1.0. While the gradient formulation reveals competing marginal incentives and the tie-balance condition characterises stationary weights, the qualitative architecture induced by these constraints remains underexplored.

The present paper addresses this omission.

## 

## 1.1.2 From Optimality to Architecture

Within the operator framework introduced in Section 1.0, the central question of this paper is:

> What structural patterns are implied by equilibrium under the Network Potential Functional?

This question is distinct from the existence or stability questions addressed previously. It concerns not whether maximisers exist, but what qualitative structure they impose on weighted networks.

In classical optimisation theory, once a functional is defined, subsequent work studies the geometry of its maximisers and the bifurcation phenomena arising under perturbation (Rockafellar, 1970; Boyd and Vandenberghe, 2004). Similarly, after Nash (1951) introduced equilibrium, later work investigated refinements, dynamics, and structural implications.

We adopt an analogous progression. The gradient and balance equations derived in Mack (2026) are treated not merely as analytic tools but as structural constraints on admissible architectures.

We show that optimisation under the Network Potential Functional is generically differentiating: it induces non-uniform weight distributions across incident edges, partitions ties into structural roles, and responds to topological shocks by breaking local symmetries.

## 

## 1.1.3 Main Contributions

The principal contributions of this paper are fourfold.

**(1) Equilibrium Role Differentiation**

We prove that at strict local maxima, incident edges are monotonically ordered with respect to brokerage exposure and redundancy participation. This ordering induces a natural partition of ties into:

- backbone edges (cohesion-dominant),

- strategic bridges (brokerage-dominant),

- redundant filaments (closure-dominated).

This classification is not imposed exogenously but emerges directly from the equilibrium conditions.

**(2) Brokerage-Induced Symmetry Breaking**

We show that the introduction of a high-brokerage edge into an otherwise equilibrated network produces a loss of stationarity and induces re-optimisation. The resulting configuration exhibits increased dispersion of incident weights and role re-sorting among neighbouring ties.

Thus, topological perturbation acts as a symmetry-breaking mechanism in the variational landscape.

**(3) Variance Expansion**

We demonstrate that, generically, brokerage shocks increase the variance of incident tie strengths at the affected node. This establishes a formal connection between topological expansion and structural complexity, measured not by edge count but by weight differentiation.

**(4) Architectural Lock-In**

Strict local maximality implies that differentiated configurations are locally persistent. Small perturbations decay under ascent dynamics, yielding stable architectural regimes.

**Local Curvature Framework**

The analysis is governed locally by the incident Hessian operator, defined as the restriction of the full Hessian operator of the Network Potential Functional to incident tie spaces. This operator represents the local curvature of the relational potential and determines rigidity, stability, and comparative statics of equilibrium tie strengths.

Local relational rigidity corresponds to invertibility of the incident Hessian operator, while structural ordering and perturbation responses are governed by its curvature and coupling structure.

## 

## 1.1.4 Conceptual Interpretation

The results establish that Network Potential optimisation is not merely a scalar maximisation problem but a **variational structural generative principle**.

Under equilibrium:

- cohesion concentrates weight into backbone supports,

- brokerage maintains weak bridges,

- redundancy suppresses over-investment in closed clusters.

Under topological expansion:

- the network reorganises,

- structural roles polarise,

- and new equilibrium architectures stabilise.

Thus, optimisation under the Network Potential Functional produces differentiated relational geometry.

## 

## 1.1.5 Position Within the Series

This paper constitutes Paper I of *The Network Potential Series*. It focuses exclusively on the structural consequences of equilibrium under fixed topology, with controlled topological perturbations.

Subsequent papers address:

- explicit allocation constraints,

- gradient-flow dynamics and convergence theory,

- endogenous topology and mixed discrete–continuous optimisation,

- and generalised structural measures.

The present work therefore provides the architectural foundation for the broader research programme.

# 1.2 Preliminaries and Notation

This section fixes notation and formalises the analytic framework required for the structural differentiation and symmetry-breaking results that follow. The analysis is conducted within the operator framework introduced in Section 1.0, in which local structural behaviour is governed by the Hessian operator and its incident restrictions. In particular, the incident Hessian operators defined in Section 1.0.2 determine local curvature, structural ordering, and perturbation response of equilibrium tie strengths.

We restate the weighted graph model and the Network Potential Functional (NPF), isolate its derivative structure, define structural indices, and formalise perturbations of topology. All assumptions are stated explicitly.

Throughout, all graphs are finite, simple, and undirected.

## 1.2.1 Weighted Graph Framework

Let

``` math
G = (V,E)
```

be a finite graph with vertex set

``` math
V = \left\{ 1,\ldots,n \right\}
```

and edge set

``` math
E \subset \left\{ \left\{ i,j \right\}:i \neq j \right\}.
```

A weight assignment is a vector

``` math
w = (w_{e})_{e \in E} \in \lbrack 0,1\rbrack^{E}.
```

For $`e = \left\{ i,j \right\}`$, we write $`w_{ij}: = w_{e}`$.

The feasible weight space is the compact hypercube

``` math
\mathcal{W} ≔ \lbrack 0,1\rbrack^{E} \subset \mathbb{R}^{\mid E \mid},
```

and we work primarily on the open set

``` math
\mathcal{W}^{\circ} ≔ (0,1)^{E},
```

where all derivatives are well-defined.

For each vertex $`i \in V`$, define:

- Neighbourhood:

``` math
N(i) ≔ \left\{ j \in V:\left\{ i,j \right\} \in E \right\},
```

- Degree:

``` math
d_{i} ≔ \mid N(i) \mid ,
```

- Incident weight vector:

``` math
w_{i} ≔ (w_{ij})_{j \in N(i)}.
```

## 1.2.2 Network Potential Functional

We restate the Network Potential Functional (NPF) introduced in Mack (2026) in schematic form sufficient for the structural analysis that follows.

Let

``` math
\Phi_{G}:\lbrack 0,1\rbrack^{E}\mathbb{\rightarrow R}
```

be a twice continuously differentiable functional of the form

``` math
\Phi_{G}(w) = \sum_{i \in V}^{}\phi_{i}(w),
```

where each node potential admits the decomposition

``` math
\phi_{i}(w) = C_{i}\left( w_{i} \right) + M_{i}(G,w) - P_{i}(G,w).
```

Here:

- $`C_{i}`$is a cohesion (stability) term depending only on incident weights $`w_{i}`$,

- $`M_{i}`$is a brokerage (mobility) term depending on structural separation,

- $`P_{i}`$is a redundancy (closure) penalty depending on weighted closure.

Assumption 2.1 (Regularity)

``` math
\Phi_{G} \in C^{2}\left( \mathcal{W}^{\circ} \right).
```

All first and second partial derivatives with respect to each $`w_{e}`$exist and are continuous on $`\mathcal{W}^{\circ}`$.

Edgewise Derivative Decomposition

For each edge $`e \in E`$, define

``` math
\partial_{e}\Phi_{G}(w) ≔ \frac{\partial}{\partial w_{e}}\Phi_{G}(w).
```

We assume the decomposition

``` math
\begin{matrix}
 & \partial_{e}\Phi_{G}(w) = C_{e}'\left( w_{e} \right) + M_{e}'(G,w) - P_{e}'(G,w), & & \text{(2.1)}
\end{matrix}
```

where:

- $`C_{e}'\left( w_{e} \right)`$depends only on $`w_{e}`$,

- $`M_{e}'(G,w)`$depends on structural separation and possibly on other weights,

- $`P_{e}'(G,w)`$depends on closure participation involving $`e`$.

- 

Equation (2.1) is the analytic foundation of the ordering and perturbation arguments.

Assumption 2.2 (Strict Cohesion Concavity)

For every edge $`e`$,

``` math
C_{e}'\left( w_{e} \right) > 0,C_{e}^{''}\left( w_{e} \right) < 0,\forall w_{e} \in (0,1).
```

Thus, cohesion is strictly increasing and strictly concave.

Assumption 2.3 (Brokerage Structure)

For every edge $`e`$:

1.  Weak-tie effect (own-weight):

- 

``` math
\frac{\partial}{\partial w_{e}}M_{e}'(G,w) < 0,
```

2.  **Structural separation effect:** for a brokerage index $`B_{e}`$,

- 

``` math
\frac{\partial}{\partial B_{e}}M_{e}'(G,w) > 0.
```

Assumption 2.4 (Redundancy Penalty Structure)

For every edge $`e`$, with redundancy index $`R_{e}`$,

``` math
P_{e}'(G,w) \geq 0,\frac{\partial}{\partial R_{e}}P_{e}'(G,w) > 0.
```

Assumption 2.5 (Non-Degenerate Curvature at Equilibrium)

At any strict local maximiser $`w^{\star} \in \mathcal{W}^{\circ}`$,

``` math
v^{\top}D^{2}\Phi_{G}\left( w^{\star} \right)v < 0\forall v \neq 0.
```

Thus, the Hessian is negative definite at strict local maxima.

## 1.2.3 Equilibrium and Stability

Definition 2.1 (Stationary Point)

A weight vector $`w \in \Omega^{\circ}`$is stationary if

``` math
\nabla\Phi(w) = 0,
```

equivalently,

``` math
\partial_{e}\Phi(w) = 0\text{for all }e \in E.
```

Definition 2.2 (Strict Local Maximiser)

A stationary point $`w^{\text{\textbackslash*}} \in \Omega^{\circ}`$is a strict local maximiser if there exists $`\varepsilon > 0`$ such that

``` math
\Phi(w) < \Phi\left( w^{\text{\textbackslash*}} \right)\text{for all }w \in \Omega\text{ with }0 < \parallel w - w^{\text{\textbackslash*}} \parallel < \varepsilon.
```

Although the feasible weight space

``` math
\Omega = \lbrack 0,1\rbrack^{\mid E \mid}
```

is compact, the structural analysis developed in this paper relies on differential properties that hold on the open domain

``` math
\Omega^{\circ} = (0,1)^{\mid E \mid}.
```

The following lemma establishes that strict local maximisers necessarily lie in the interior of the feasible region under the marginal structure of the Network Potential Functional.

Lemma 2.2 (Interior Optimality)

Let

``` math
\Phi:\Omega \rightarrow \mathbb{R}
```

be twice continuously differentiable on

``` math
\Omega^{\circ}.
```

Suppose that for each edge $`e \in E`$, the partial derivative admits a decomposition

``` math
\partial_{e}\Phi(w) = M_{e}(w) - P_{e}(w),
```

where:

1.  Lower Boundary Divergence

``` math
\underset{w_{e} \rightarrow 0^{+}}{lim}M_{e}(w_{e},w_{- e}) = + \infty,
```

uniformly in the remaining coordinates $`w_{- e}`$.

That is, for every compact set $`K \subset (0,1\rbrack^{m - 1}`$,

``` math
\underset{w_{e} \rightarrow 0^{+}}{\lim}\text{\:\,}\underset{w_{- e} \in K}{\inf}M_{e}(w_{e},w_{- e}) = + \infty.
```

2.  Upper Boundary Descent

- 

There exist constants $`\delta > 0`$and $`c_{e} > 0`$ such that

``` math
\frac{\partial\Phi_{G}}{\partial w_{e}}(w) \leq - c_{e}
```

whenever

``` math
{1 - \delta < w_{e} \leq 1.
}
```
uniformly in $`w_{- e}`$.

Then every local maximiser $`w^{\text{\textbackslash*}}`$of $`\Phi`$ satisfies

``` math
w^{\text{\textbackslash*}} \in \Omega^{\circ}.
```

Proof

Suppose, for contradiction, that $`w^{\star}`$is a local maximiser of $`\Phi_{G}`$ on the hypercube $`\Omega: = \lbrack 0,1\rbrack^{\mid E \mid}`$, and that there exists an edge $`e \in E`$ such that

``` math
w_{e}^{\star} = 0.
```

By the Lower Boundary Divergence condition,

``` math
\underset{w_{e} \rightarrow 0^{+}}{\lim}\frac{\partial\Phi_{G}}{\partial w_{e}}(w_{e},w_{- e}) = + \infty,
```

uniformly in the remaining coordinates $`w_{- e}`$.

In particular, fixing $`w_{- e} = w_{- e}^{\star}`$, there exists $`\varepsilon > 0`$such that

``` math
\frac{\partial\Phi_{G}}{\partial w_{e}}(w_{e},w_{- e}^{\star}) > 0\text{whenever}0 < w_{e} < \varepsilon.
```

Consider the direction $`e_{e} \in \mathbb{R}^{\mid E \mid}`$ corresponding to increasing the $`e`$-th coordinate. For sufficiently small $`t > 0`$, the point

``` math
w^{\star} + te_{e}
```

remains feasible and satisfies $`0 < t < \varepsilon`$. By the fundamental theorem of calculus,

``` math
\Phi_{G}(w^{\star} + te_{e}) - \Phi_{G}(w^{\star}) = \int_{0}^{t}\frac{\partial\Phi_{G}}{\partial w_{e}}(w^{\star} + se_{e})\text{ }ds.
```

Since the integrand is strictly positive for sufficiently small $`s`$, the integral is strictly positive, and therefore

``` math
\Phi_{G}(w^{\star} + te_{e}) > \Phi_{G}(w^{\star}),
```

contradicting local maximality of $`w^{\star}`$.

Now suppose instead that

``` math
w_{e}^{\star} = 1.
```

By the Upper Boundary Descent condition, there exist constants $`\delta > 0`$and $`c_{e} > 0`$such that

``` math
\frac{\partial\Phi_{G}}{\partial w_{e}}(w) \leq - c_{e} < 0
```

whenever $`1 - \delta < w_{e} < 1`$, uniformly in the remaining coordinates.

Fixing $`w_{- e} = w_{- e}^{\star}`$, choose $`0 < t < \delta`$. Then

``` math
w^{\star} - te_{e}
```

remains feasible, and by the fundamental theorem of calculus,

``` math
\Phi_{G}(w^{\star} - te_{e}) - \Phi_{G}(w^{\star}) = - \int_{0}^{t}\frac{\partial\Phi_{G}}{\partial w_{e}}(w^{\star} - se_{e})\text{ }ds.
```

Since the integrand is bounded above by a strictly negative constant, the integral is negative, and hence

``` math
\Phi_{G}(w^{\star} - te_{e}) > \Phi_{G}(w^{\star}),
```

again, contradicting local maximality.

Therefore, neither boundary case can occur. It follows that

``` math
0 < w_{e}^{\star} < 1\text{for all }e \in E,
```

and hence

``` math
w^{\star} \in \Omega^{\circ},
```

the interior of the hypercube.

Corollary 2.3 (Stationarity of Maximisers)

Every strict local maximiser $`w^{\text{\textbackslash*}}`$of the Network Potential Functional satisfies

``` math
\nabla\Phi\left( w^{\text{\textbackslash*}} \right) = 0.
```

Accordingly, the structural analysis in the remainder of this paper restricts attention to interior stationary points.

The ordering and structural differentiation results developed in later sections therefore apply to interior stationary configurations.

## 1.2.8 Local Relational Rigidity and Cross-Partial Control

The structural ordering results developed in this paper depend on the stability of the marginal incentive structure at an interior stationary point. Because incident edge weights interact through brokerage and redundancy effects, the marginal condition for one edge generally depends on the weights of neighbouring edges. A stability condition is therefore required to ensure that direct marginal effects dominate indirect coupling effects.

### 1.2.8.1 Incident Hessian Structure

Let $`i \in V`$and let

``` math
E_{i} = \{ e_{1},\ldots,e_{k}\}
```

denote the set of edges incident to vertex $`i`$.

Define the **incident Hessian block**

``` math
H_{i}(w) = \left( \frac{\partial^{2}\Phi(w)}{\partial w_{e_{a}}\text{ }\partial w_{e_{b}}} \right)_{a,b = 1}^{k}.
```

The entries of $`H_{i}(w)`$describe the local interaction structure among weights incident to node $`i`$.

Diagonal entries measure the direct curvature of the potential with respect to individual edge weights:

``` math
\frac{\partial^{2}\Phi}{\partial w_{e}^{2}}.
```

Off-diagonal entries measure cross-partial interactions:

``` math
\frac{\partial^{2}\Phi}{\partial w_{e}\text{ }\partial w_{e'}}.
```

These cross-partials arise from brokerage and redundancy interactions and represent indirect coupling between incident ties.

The incident Hessian therefore captures the local feedback structure governing equilibrium adjustments among neighbouring ties.

The matrix $`H_{i}(w)`$ provides a coordinate representation of the local curvature of the Network Potential Functional on the incident weight space. In §1.2.8.2 this object will be interpreted canonically as the restriction of the global Hessian operator to the incident subspace.

## 1.2.8.2 The Incident Hessian Operator

While the incident Hessian $`H_{i}(w)`$ has been introduced as a matrix block describing the local interaction structure among weights incident to node $`i`$, it is conceptually useful to interpret this object canonically as a linear operator acting on the incident weight space. This operator formulation clarifies the roles of rigidity, monotonicity, and comparative statics in the analysis that follows.

Let $`E(i) \subseteq E`$ denote the set of edges incident to node $`i`$, and let

``` math
\mathbb{R}^{E(i)}
```

denote the corresponding incident weight space. Elements of $`\mathbb{R}^{E(i)}`$ represent perturbations of tie strengths affecting only edges incident to node $`i`$.

Let

``` math
H(w) = D^{2}\Phi_{G}(w)
```

denote the global Hessian of the Network Potential Functional, viewed as a linear operator

``` math
H(w):\mathbb{R}^{E} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{E}.
```

Let

``` math
P_{i}:\mathbb{R}^{E} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{E(i)}
```

denote the coordinate projection onto incident edges and let

``` math
\iota_{i}:\mathbb{R}^{E(i)} \hookrightarrow \mathbb{R}^{E}
```

denote the natural inclusion map.

**Definition 1.5 (Incident Hessian Operator)**

The **incident Hessian operator** at node $`i`$is defined by

``` math
\mathcal{H}_{i}(w) = P_{i}\text{ }H(w)\text{ }\iota_{i}:\mathbb{R}^{E(i)} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{E(i)}.
```

Equivalently, $`\mathcal{H}_{i}(w)`$ is represented in edge coordinates by the principal Hessian block $`H_{i}(w)`$ introduced in §1.2.8.1. The operator formulation makes explicit that the incident Hessian acts intrinsically on the incident perturbation space and is independent of the ordering of edges.

### 

**Structural Interpretation**

The operator $`\mathcal{H}_{i}(w)`$represents the local curvature of the Network Potential Functional with respect to perturbations of tie strengths incident to node $`i`$. In particular:

- diagonal entries describe marginal curvature of individual ties,

- off-diagonal entries describe cross-partial coupling between incident ties,

- the operator spectrum determines local curvature behaviour on the incident subspace and therefore governs local structural stability and comparative statics.

Thus $`\mathcal{H}_{i}(w)`$ governs the local structural response of equilibrium tie strengths to perturbations of incident marginal incentives.

### 

**Relationship to Marginal Maps**

Let

``` math
M_{i}(w) = P_{i}\nabla\Phi_{G}(w)
```

denote the incident marginal map introduced in §1.2.8.3. Its Jacobian satisfies

``` math
DM_{i}(w) = \mathcal{H}_{i}(w).
```

Thus, the incident Hessian operator is precisely the Jacobian of the incident marginal map.

This identity provides the analytic basis for the monotonicity interpretation developed below.

### 

**Role in Rigidity and Comparative Statics**

At an interior equilibrium $`w^{\star}`$satisfying

``` math
\nabla\Phi_{G}(w^{\star}) = 0,
```

local comparative statics of incident tie strengths are governed by the operator equation

``` math
\Delta w_{E(i)} = - \mathcal{H}_{i}(w^{\star})^{- 1}\text{ }\Delta M_{i},
```

whenever $`\mathcal{H}_{i}(w^{\star})`$is invertible.

Invertibility of the incident Hessian operator therefore provides a natural notion of **local relational rigidity**, ensuring that equilibrium incident weights respond uniquely and continuously to perturbations of marginal incentives.

The diagonal dominance condition introduced in Assumption 2.6 below provides a sufficient condition for invertibility of $`\mathcal{H}_{i}(w^{\star})`$.

### 

**Canonical Status**

The operator $`\mathcal{H}_{i}(w)`$is the restriction of the global Hessian $`D^{2}\Phi_{G}(w)`$to the incident weight space and therefore represents the intrinsic second-variation operator governing local curvature of the relational potential.

This operator will serve as the fundamental local curvature object underlying rigidity, structural ordering, and comparative statics results throughout the remainder of the paper, while the global Hessian operator governs full-network curvature and stability.

## 1.2.8.3 Assumption 2.6 (Local Relational Rigidity)

At an interior stationary point $`w^{\text{\textbackslash*}}`$, local relational rigidity is characterised by invertibility of the incident Hessian operator $`\mathcal{H}_{i}(w^{\text{\textbackslash*}})`$ introduced in §1.2.8.2 representing nonsingularity of the Hessian operator restricted to the incident weight space. Assumption 2.6 provides a sufficient condition for this property expressed in terms of strict diagonal dominance of the incident Hessian matrix.

### Assumption 2.6 (Local Relational Rigidity)

At an interior stationary point $`w^{\text{\textbackslash*}}`$, the incident Hessian block satisfies strict diagonal dominance:

``` math
\mid \frac{\partial^{2}\Phi}{\partial w_{e}^{2}}(w^{\text{\textbackslash*}}) \mid > \sum_{e' \neq e}^{}{\mid \frac{\partial^{2}\Phi}{\partial w_{e}\partial w_{e'}}(w^{\text{\textbackslash*}}) \mid}
```

for all $`e \in E_{i}`$.

This condition ensures that the marginal effect of an edge on its own incentive dominates the indirect feedback induced by neighbouring edges.

Under Assumption 2.6 the incident Hessian matrix $`H_{i}(w^{\text{\textbackslash*}})`$, and equivalently the incident Hessian operator $`\mathcal{H}_{i}(w^{\text{\textbackslash*}})`$, is nonsingular. The equilibrium system for incident weights is therefore locally rigid in the sense that perturbations of marginal incentives determine unique equilibrium responses.

Assumption 2.6 will be referred to as the **local rigidity condition**.

## 

## 1.2.8.4 Strong Monotonicity Interpretation

Define the incident marginal map

``` math
M_{i}(w) = (\partial_{e}\Phi(w))_{e \in E_{i}}.
```

The Jacobian of this map at a stationary point satisfies

``` math
DM_{i}(w^{\text{\textbackslash*}}) = \mathcal{H}_{i}(w^{\text{\textbackslash*}}),
```

equivalently represented in coordinates by

``` math
DM_{i}(w^{\text{\textbackslash*}}) = H_{i}(w^{\text{\textbackslash*}}).
```

Under Assumption 2.6 the incident Hessian operator $`\mathcal{H}_{i}(w^{\text{\textbackslash*}})`$is nonsingular by the Gershgorin circle theorem.

Consequently:

- the incident equilibrium system is locally well-posed,

- stationary solutions are locally unique,

- marginal incentives vary continuously with structural perturbations,

- and comparative statics are well-defined via the Implicit Function Theorem.

In particular, the inverse operator $`\mathcal{H}_{i}(w^{\text{\textbackslash*}})^{- 1}`$exists and provides a linear response approximation

``` math
\Delta w_{i} = - \mathcal{H}_{i}(w^{\text{\textbackslash*}})^{- 1}\Delta M_{i},
```

equivalently,

``` math
\Delta w_{i} = - H_{i}(w^{\text{\textbackslash*}})^{- 1}\Delta M_{i}.
```

This representation will be used in Section 5 to analyse structural perturbations.

In this sense, Assumption 2.6 imposes a local strong-monotonicity condition on the incident marginal map, ensuring that structural signals are not overwhelmed by indirect coupling effects.

## 

## 1.2.8.5 Relation to M-Matrix Conditions

Proposition 2.2 provides an explicit parameter regime under which Assumption 2.6 holds for the Network Potential Functional.

In this regime:

- diagonal entries of $`H_{i}`$are negative,

- off-diagonal entries are non-positive,

- and strict diagonal dominance holds.

Under these conditions

``` math
- H_{i}(w^{\text{\textbackslash*}})
```

is an M-matrix.

Equivalently, the operator

``` math
- \mathcal{H}_{i}(w^{\text{\textbackslash*}})
```

is a monotone inverse-positive operator on the incident weight space.

This guarantees:

- invertibility,

- monotone comparative statics,

- and sign control of the equilibrium response.

In particular, structural index differences

``` math
\sigma(e_{1}) \prec \sigma(e_{2})
```

translate into monotone weight differences

``` math
w_{e_{1}}^{\text{\textbackslash*}} > w_{e_{2}}^{\text{\textbackslash*}}.
```

This property forms the basis for the ordering theorem of Section 4.

## 

## 1.2.8.6 Behaviour Outside the Rigidity Regime

Assumption 2.6 is sufficient but not necessary for the existence of stationary points.

When the local rigidity condition fails, the incident Hessian operator $`\mathcal{H}_{i}(w^{\text{\textbackslash*}})`$may become singular or nearly singular. In such cases:

- stationary points may still exist,

- the incident system may remain solvable,

- but monotone ordering of incident weights is no longer guaranteed.

In non-rigid regimes:

- multiple local maxima may arise,

- comparative statics may become non-monotone,

- and the structural role strata defined in Section 1.4 may become unstable or overlapping.

Paper I therefore studies the rigidity regime as the domain in which structural differentiation is provably determined by the Network Potential Functional.

The architectural ordering results developed in Section 1.4, and in particular Theorem 4.1, rely on the rigidity condition to ensure that structural index differences produce monotone differences in equilibrium weights.

# 1.3 Equilibrium Tie-Balance Revisited

Within the operator framework introduced in Section 1.0, interior equilibria of admissible relational potentials satisfy first-order stationarity conditions determined by the gradient operator and second-order structure determined by the Hessian operator. In particular, equilibrium tie strengths are governed locally by the incident Hessian operators and their associated equilibrium response properties.

For the Network Potential Functional (Mack, 2026), interior equilibria satisfy balance conditions equating marginal cohesion and brokerage gains with marginal redundancy penalties. These conditions determine stationary tie strengths and provide the analytic basis for structural ordering relations among incident edges.

In the present paper, these equilibrium conditions are interpreted through the incident Hessian operators and the associated structural indices defined in Section 1.0.3. The tie-balance equations are therefore treated not merely as scalar equilibrium conditions but as constraints determining the local operator geometry of equilibrium configurations. Ordering relations among incident edges arise from anisotropy of the equilibrium response operator and are governed by the curvature structure of the relational potential.

All results in this section rely on the derivative decomposition (2.1), Assumptions 2.1–2.5, the local relational rigidity condition Assumption 2.6, and the operator framework introduced in Section 1.0.

## 1.3.1 First-Order Condition and Implicit Determination

Let $`G = (V,E)`$ be fixed and let $`w^{\star} \in (0,1)^{E}`$ be a stationary point of $`\Phi_{G}`$. Then for each edge $`e \in E`$,

``` math
\partial_{e}\Phi_{G}\left( w^{\star} \right) = 0.
```

By (2.1),

``` math
\begin{matrix}
 & C_{e}'\left( w_{e}^{\star} \right) + M_{e}'\left( G,w^{\star} \right) - P_{e}'\left( G,w^{\star} \right) = 0. & & \text{(3.1)}
\end{matrix}
```

Equivalently,

``` math
\begin{matrix}
 & C_{e}'\left( w_{e}^{\star} \right) = P_{e}'\left( G,w^{\star} \right) - M_{e}'\left( G,w^{\star} \right). & & \text{(3.2)}
\end{matrix}
```

Lemma 3.0 (Implicit Determination of Equilibrium Weights)

Under Assumptions 2.2 and 2.5, for each edge $`e`$, equation (3.2) locally determines the equilibrium weight $`w_{e}^{\star}`$ as a continuously differentiable function of

``` math
\Theta_{e} ≔ P_{e}'\left( G,w^{\star} \right) - M_{e}'\left( G,w^{\star} \right)
```

in a neighbourhood of any strict local maximiser.

Proof.

At a strict local maximiser $`w^{\star}`$, the first-order condition (3.2) may be written as

``` math
C_{e}'\left( w_{e}^{\star} \right) = \Theta_{e}.
```

By Assumption 2.2, the cohesion marginal $`C_{e}'\left( w_{e} \right)`$ is continuously differentiable and strictly monotone on $`\left( 0,1 \right)`$, so that

``` math
C_{e}^{''}\left( w_{e}^{\star} \right) \neq 0.
```

Therefore, the derivative of the marginal condition with respect to $`\mathbf{w}_{\mathbf{e}}`$is nonzero at equilibrium. The Implicit Function Theorem then implies that the equation

``` math
C_{e}'\left( w_{e} \right) = \Theta_{e}
```

can be solved locally for $`w_{e}`$as a continuously differentiable function of $`\Theta_{e}`$ in a neighbourhood of $`w_{e}^{\star}`$.

## 1.3.2 Multivariate Comparative Statics on the Incident System

Comparative statics of equilibrium tie strengths are governed by the equilibrium response operator introduced in Section 1.0.2 and formalised as the **susceptibility kernel** in Definition 3.2.

Let $`\Phi_{\lambda}`$ be a smooth parameterised family of relational potentials depending on a scalar parameter $`\lambda`$. Suppose that for $`\lambda`$in a neighbourhood of $`\lambda_{0}`$, there exists an interior equilibrium configuration

``` math
w^{*}(\lambda) \in W^{\circ}
```

satisfying the stationarity condition

``` math
\nabla\Phi_{\lambda}(w^{*}(\lambda)) = 0.
```

Assume that the Hessian operator

``` math
H(w^{*}) = D^{2}\Phi_{\lambda_{0}}(w^{*}(\lambda_{0}))
```

is nonsingular, and let

``` math
K = H(w^{*})^{- 1}
```

denote the susceptibility kernel.

Then the Implicit Function Theorem implies that the equilibrium mapping

``` math
\lambda \mapsto w^{*}(\lambda)
```

is differentiable in a neighbourhood of $`\lambda_{0}`$.

Differentiating the stationarity condition yields the equilibrium response equation

``` math
H(w^{*})\frac{dw^{*}}{d\lambda} = - \frac{\partial}{\partial\lambda}\nabla\Phi_{\lambda}(w^{*}).
```

Solving for the equilibrium response gives

``` math
\frac{dw^{*}}{d\lambda} = - K\frac{\partial}{\partial\lambda}\nabla\Phi_{\lambda}(w^{*}).
```

Thus first-order variation of equilibrium tie strengths is determined by the susceptibility kernel $`K`$, which governs the propagation of perturbations through the equilibrium configuration.

**Incident Comparative Statics**

Local structural responses are determined by the incident components of the equilibrium response vector. For each node $`i \in V`$and each edge $`e \in \delta(i)`$,

``` math
\frac{dw_{e}^{*}}{d\lambda} = - \left( K\frac{\partial}{\partial\lambda}\nabla\Phi_{\lambda}(w^{*}) \right)_{e}.
```

Differences in equilibrium responses across incident edges arise from anisotropy of the susceptibility kernel. In particular, distinct rows of $`K`$ induce distinct sensitivities to perturbation parameters.

Consequently, structural differentiation of incident edges corresponds to anisotropic equilibrium responses governed by the susceptibility kernel.

## 1.3.3 Structural Dominance and Partial Ordering

Definition 3.1 (Structural Dominance)

For incident edges $`e,f \in E(i)`$ , we say $`e`$structurally dominates $`f\`$if

``` math
B_{e} \geq B_{f},R_{e} \geq R_{f},
```

with at least one strict inequality.

Theorem 3.1 (Equilibrium Structural Ordering)

Let $`w^{\star}`$ be a strict local maximiser. Under Assumption 2.6, if $`e`$structurally dominates $`f`$, then

``` math
w_{e}^{\star} < w_{f}^{\star}
```

generically (i.e., outside the structural symmetry locus).

Remark 3.1 (M-matrix Sign Control)

With the M-matrix sign condition (Proposition 2.2), the ordering admits sign-preserving comparative statics: exposure increases induce weight decreases without requiring magnitude thresholds.

Remark 3.2 (Partial Order Structure)

Structural dominance defines a strict partial order. Incomparability may occur when brokerage and redundancy move in opposite directions; in such cases Theorem 3.1 yields no ordering conclusion.

## 1.3.4 Consequences for Role Differentiation

Theorem 3.1 implies that equilibrium weights respect structural exposure. Extremal comparisons yield:

- Edges with minimal $`\left( B_{e},R_{e} \right)`$ attain maximal incident weights,

- High-brokerage edges (with low redundancy) attain strictly lower weights,

- High-redundancy edges attain minimal weights among incident ties.

These statements are made precise in §1.4.

## 1.3.5 Local Rigidity

By Assumption 2.6 and Lemma 2.1, the incident stationary system is locally well-posed and stable to small perturbations: strict inequalities persist in a neighbourhood of the equilibrium. Thus, structural ordering is locally robust to cross-partial feedback.

## 1.3.6 Summary

Equation (3.1) implicitly determines equilibrium weights as monotone functions of structural exposure. Incorporating cross-partial control (Assumption 2.6), these monotonicity statements remain valid in the fully coupled incident system.

Therefore, equilibrium under the Network Potential Functional is structurally discriminating in a robust multivariate sense: heterogeneity in brokerage and redundancy exposure induces heterogeneous tie strengths, and this ordering cannot be overturned by incident feedback under the local rigidity regime.

# 1.4 Structural Differentiation

## 1.4.1 Overview

Section 1.3 established that equilibrium weights vary smoothly with structural perturbations and that equilibrium responses are governed by the inverse incident Hessian operator

# 1.5 Brokerage Shocks and Symmetry Breaking

## 1.5.1 Overview

Section 1.4 established that structural asymmetry generically induces strict differentiation of incident weights at equilibrium. We now examine how equilibrium responds to a *topological perturbation*, specifically the addition of a new edge that introduces brokerage exposure.

The principal goals of this section are to show:

1.  Equilibrium persists smoothly under small topological perturbations.

2.  Brokerage shocks generically break structural symmetry.

3.  Local dispersion of tie strengths increases under non-degenerate perturbations.

Throughout, Assumptions 2.1–2.6 hold.

## 1.5.2 Parametric Formulation of Edge Addition

Let $`G = (V,E)`$ and let $`e_{0} = \left\{ i,j \right\} \notin E`$.

Define the perturbed graph:

``` math
G' = \left( V,E \cup \left\{ e_{0} \right\} \right).
```

We model the incremental brokerage contribution induced by the new edge via a smooth perturbation parameter $`\lambda \geq 0`$.

Define a perturbed functional:

``` math
\begin{matrix}
 & \Phi_{G'}(w,\lambda) = \Phi_{G}(w) + \lambda\Psi(w), & & \text{(5.1)}
\end{matrix}
```

where:

- $`w \in (0,1)^{E'}`$,

- $`\Psi \in C^{2}((0,1)^{E'})`$,

- $`\Psi`$captures the brokerage contribution induced by structural separation through $`e_{0}`$,

- $`\Psi`$vanishes identically when structural separation is absent.

- 

Assume smooth dependence jointly in $`\left( w,\lambda \right)`$.

## 1.5.3 Gradient Map and Equilibrium Equation

Define the gradient map

``` math
F(w,\lambda) ≔ \nabla_{w}\Phi_{G'}(w,\lambda) \in \mathbb{R}^{\mid E' \mid}.
```

Equilibrium is characterised by

``` math
\begin{matrix}
 & F(w,\lambda) = 0. & & \text{(5.2)}
\end{matrix}
```

Let $`w^{\star} \in (0,1)^{E}`$ be a strict local maximiser of $`\Phi_{G}`$.

Embed it into the expanded space as

``` math
{\widetilde{w}}^{\star} ≔ \left( w^{\star},0 \right).
```

Then

``` math
F\left( {\widetilde{w}}^{\star},0 \right) = 0.
```

## 1.5.4 Persistence of Equilibrium

Theorem 5.1 (Smooth Persistence Under Edge Addition)

Suppose $`D_{w}F\left( {\widetilde{w}}^{\star},0 \right)`$ is invertible.\
Then there exists $`\varepsilon > 0`$and a unique smooth mapping

``` math
\lambda \mapsto w^{\star}(\lambda)
```

defined for $`\left. \ \lambda \in \lbrack 0,\varepsilon \right)`$ such that

``` math
F\left( w^{\star}(\lambda),\lambda \right) = 0,w^{\star}(0) = {\widetilde{w}}^{\star}.
```

Proof

Compute the Jacobian:

``` math
D_{w}F\left( {\widetilde{w}}^{\star},0 \right) = D^{2}\Phi_{G}\left( w^{\star} \right).
```

By Assumption 2.5, this Hessian is negative definite and therefore invertible.

Since $`F \in C^{1}`$ jointly in $`\left( w,\lambda \right)`$ , the Implicit Function Theorem applies, yielding existence and uniqueness of a smooth equilibrium branch $`w^{\star}(\lambda)`$.

## 1.5.5 Linear Response Formula

Differentiating (5.2) with respect to $`\lambda`$,

``` math
D_{w}F\left( w^{\star}(0),0 \right)\frac{dw^{\star}}{d\lambda} + \partial_{\lambda}F\left( w^{\star}(0),0 \right) = 0.
```

Hence,

``` math
\begin{matrix}
 & \frac{dw^{\star}}{d\lambda} = - (D^{2}\Phi_{G}(w^{\star}))^{- 1}\text{ }\partial_{\lambda}F(w^{\star},0). & & \text{(5.3)}
\end{matrix}
```

Equation (5.3) gives the first-order response of equilibrium weights.

## 1.5.6 Symmetry Breaking

Suppose vertex $`i`$has two incident edges $`e,f \in E(i)`$ such that

``` math
\sigma(e) = \sigma(f)
```

at $`\lambda = 0`$. Then $`w_{e}^{\star}(0) = w_{f}^{\star}(0)`$.

Assume the perturbation $`\Psi`$modifies brokerage exposure asymmetrically:

``` math
\begin{matrix}
 & \partial_{\lambda}F_{e}\left( w^{\star},0 \right) \neq \partial_{\lambda}F_{f}\left( w^{\star},0 \right). & & \text{(5.4)}
\end{matrix}
```

Theorem 5.2 (First-Order Symmetry Breaking)

Under condition (5.4),

``` math
\frac{dw_{e}^{\star}}{d\lambda} \neq \frac{dw_{f}^{\star}}{d\lambda}.
```

Consequently, for all sufficiently small $`\lambda > 0`$,

``` math
w_{e}^{\star}(\lambda) \neq w_{f}^{\star}(\lambda).
```

Proof

Let $`w^{\star}(\lambda)`$denote the equilibrium branch defined by the stationarity conditions

``` math
F(w,\lambda) = \nabla\Phi_{G(\lambda)}(w) = 0.
```

Differentiating with respect to $`\lambda`$at $`\lambda = 0`$yields

``` math
\frac{dw^{\star}}{d\lambda} = - H^{- 1}\frac{\partial F}{\partial\lambda},
```

where

``` math
H = D^{2}\Phi_{G}\left( w^{\star} \right)
```

is the Hessian evaluated at the equilibrium $`w^{\star}`$. By Assumption 2.6, $`H`$is nonsingular and therefore invertible.

Suppose that two incident edges $`e,f \in E(i)`$satisfy identical equilibrium weights at $`\lambda = 0`$. By Lemma 5.1, the structural perturbation induces unequal marginal responses,

``` math
\frac{\partial F_{e}}{\partial\lambda} \neq \frac{\partial F_{f}}{\partial\lambda}.
```

Since $`H^{- 1}`$is invertible, the linear mapping

``` math
v \mapsto H^{- 1}v
```

is nonsingular. Equality of the components corresponding to edges $`e`$and $`f`$in the vector

``` math
- H^{- 1}\frac{\partial F}{\partial\lambda}
```

can therefore occur only if the perturbation vector $`\partial_{\lambda}F`$lies in a proper linear subspace of codimension at least one. Such perturbations form a set of Lebesgue measure zero.

Consequently,

``` math
\frac{dw_{e}^{\star}}{d\lambda} \neq \frac{dw_{f}^{\star}}{d\lambda}
```

for all perturbation directions except a measure-zero subset.

Therefore, equality of the equilibrium weights cannot persist for sufficiently small $`\lambda > 0`$, and the perturbed equilibrium satisfies

``` math
w_{e}^{\star}(\lambda) \neq w_{f}^{\star}(\lambda).
```

This establishes generic symmetry breaking under structural perturbations.

## 1.5.7 Variance Expansion

Let $`i \in V`$and define the incident variance


At $`\lambda = 0`$ , structural symmetry implies that incident symmetric edges satisfy

Hence weight differences emerge linearly in $`\lambda`$. Substituting into (5.5) and evaluating at $`\lambda = 0`$, we obtain

Under asymmetric brokerage perturbation,


Thus, dispersion of incident weights increases to first order.

## 1.6.8 Variance Persistence

Let $`i \in V`$and define the incident variance

``` math
{Var}_{i}(w) = \frac{1}{d_{i}}\sum_{j \in N(i)}^{}\left( w_{ij}-{\overset{ˉ}{w}}_{i} \right)^{2},
```

where

``` math

```

Suppose that at equilibrium

``` math
{Var}_{i}(w^{\star}) > 0.
```

Let $`w^{\star}(\lambda)`$ denote a smooth equilibrium branch under perturbation parameter $`\lambda`$, as established previously. By continuity of $`w^{\star}(\lambda)`$in $`\lambda`$, and continuity of the variance functional, it follows that

``` math
{Var}_{i}(w^{\star}(\lambda)) \rightarrow {Var}_{i}(w^{\star})\text{as }\lambda \rightarrow 0.
```

Hence there exists $`\varepsilon > 0`$ such that

``` math
\mid \lambda \mid < \varepsilon \Longrightarrow {Var}_{i}(w^{\star}(\lambda)) > 0.
```

Therefore, strictly positive dispersion of incident weights is locally structurally stable under small perturbations.

# 1.7 Geometric Structure of the Variational Problem

## 1.7.1 Functional Setting

Let

``` math
W \equiv (0,1)^{E} \subset \mathbb{R}^{\mid E \mid}
```

denote the interior of the feasible weight space.

The Network Potential Functional

``` math
\Phi_{G}:W \rightarrow \mathbb{R}
```

is twice continuously differentiable (Assumption 2.1).

Critical points satisfy

``` math
\nabla\Phi_{G}(w) = 0,
```

and their local structure is determined by the Hessian

``` math
H(w) \equiv D^{2}\Phi_{G}(w).
```

The spectrum of $`H\left( w^{\star} \right)`$ determines whether $`w^{\star}`$is:

- a strict local maximum (negative definite),

- a saddle (indefinite),

- or a degenerate critical point (nontrivial kernel).

The structural differentiation and stability results of §§1.3–1.6 apply exclusively to strict local maxima.

The geometric viewpoint developed in this section provides the variational foundation for the architectural structure described earlier. Structural dominance ordering induced by the structural index map $`\sigma`$corresponds locally to monotone ordering relations among equilibrium weights in the potential landscape, while equilibrium weights themselves correspond to stationary points of the functional.

The architectural regimes illustrated in Figure 1 arise from the interaction between first-order equilibrium conditions and second-order curvature properties of the functional.

## 1.7.2 Concavity and Global Uniqueness

If $`\Phi_{G}`$ is strictly concave on $`W`$, then:

1.  $`H(w)`$is negative definite for all $`w \in W`$,

2.  A unique global maximiser exists,

3.  Every gradient trajectory converges to that maximiser.

In this regime, structural differentiation is uniquely determined by topology.

No alternative local equilibria exist.

## 1.7.3 Non-Concave Regimes and Multiplicity

If closure interactions render $`\Phi_{G}`$ non-concave, then:

- the Hessian may be indefinite in regions of the weight space

- multiple strict local maxima may exist.

Each strict local maximiser satisfies the differentiation and stability results established in §§1.3–1.6, including the structural role strata of Definition 4.1 and the architectural ordering of Theorem 4.3. In particular, every strict local maximiser induces a structural partition of incident edges into Backbone, Bridge, and Filament strata as defined in Definition 4.1.

Multiplicity of strict local maxima therefore corresponds to the existence of multiple locally stable architectural configurations rather than to a failure of structural differentiation.

From the variational perspective, multiplicity reflects the presence of multiple isolated hyperbolic stationary points in the potential landscape. Each such stationary point represents a distinct differentiated architecture satisfying the same structural ordering principles but with different equilibrium weight magnitudes.

Thus, non-concavity produces architectural plurality rather than architectural indeterminacy.

## 1.7.4 Symmetry and Degeneracy

Let $`\Gamma`$ be a permutation group acting on coordinates corresponding to structurally identical edges (i.e., equal brokerage and redundancy indices).

If $`\Phi_{G}`$is invariant under $`\Gamma`$, then symmetric equilibria may exhibit degeneracy.

Proposition 1.7.1 (Symmetry–Degeneracy Correspondence)

Suppose $`w^{\star}`$is a critical point and $`\Phi_{G}`$is invariant under a nontrivial permutation group $`\Gamma`$acting on incident coordinates at vertex $`i`$.

Then the Hessian $`H\left( w^{\star} \right)`$admits a nontrivial nullspace containing the tangent space to the $`\Gamma`$-orbit through $`w^{\star}`$.

Proof (Sketch)

Invariance implies

``` math
\Phi_{G}(\gamma \cdot w) = \Phi_{G}(w)\forall\gamma \in \Gamma.
```

Differentiating twice along orbit directions yields zero curvature along symmetry directions.

Thus, symmetric equilibria lie on critical manifolds rather than being isolated.

## 1.7.5 Generic Lifting of Degeneracy

Let $`\mathcal{F}`$denote the space of $`C^{2}`$functionals on $`W`$, equipped with the $`C^{2}`$- topology.

Proposition 1.7.2 (Generic Hyperbolicity)

For an open dense subset of $`\mathcal{F}`$, all critical points of $`\Phi_{G}`$are non-degenerate (i.e., the Hessian is invertible).

Interpretation

Degenerate equilibria arising from structural symmetry are non-generic.\
Small $`C^{2}`$-perturbations—such as brokerage shifts—remove degeneracy and produce isolated hyperbolic equilibria.

Thus symmetry-breaking corresponds to spectral splitting of zero eigenvalues.

## 1.7.6 Curvature Anisotropy and Weight Dispersion

Let $`H_{i}`$denote the restriction of the Hessian $`\nabla^{2}\Phi_{G}(w^{\star})`$to the subspace spanned by incident edge coordinates $`\left\{ w_{e}:e \in E(i) \right\}`$.

### 

### Proposition 1.7.3 (Curvature–Dispersion Link)

Suppose $`w^{\star}`$is a strict interior local maximiser and the principal curvatures of $`H_{i}`$are not all equal. Then generically,

``` math
{Var}_{i}(w^{\star}) > 0.
```

### Proof (Sketch)

At an interior stationary point,

``` math
\frac{\partial\Phi_{G}}{\partial w_{e}}(w^{\star}) = 0\text{for all }e \in E(i).
```

Under symmetric structure, incident weights coincide and $`H_{i}`$is spectrally isotropic in the symmetric subspace. However, if the eigenvalues of $`H_{i}`$differ, then the quadratic approximation of $`\Phi_{G}`$exhibits direction-dependent curvature across incident coordinates.

As shown in Section 1.5, asymmetric perturbations generate distinct first-order responses of incident weights. These first-order differences arise precisely because the second-order geometry (the Hessian) distinguishes incident directions. Thus, curvature anisotropy supplies the mechanism underlying the variance expansion established in (5.5).

Therefore, absent exact structural symmetry or degenerate curvature, equilibrium weights cannot remain equal across incident directions. Hence

``` math
{Var}_{i}(w^{\star}) > 0.
```

### Structural Interpretation

Section 1.5 showed that asymmetric perturbations generate variance to first order.\
The present result identifies the geometric source of that effect: spectral anisotropy of the incident Hessian**.**

Thus, variance expansion is not accidental—it is a manifestation of unequal principal curvatures.

## 1.7.7 Dimensional Expansion Under Edge Addition

Let


The weight space expands from

``` math

```

### Criticality in the Expanded Space

The embedded configuration $`{\widetilde{w}}^{\star}`$is stationary in $`\mathcal{W}'`$if and only if

``` math
\frac{\partial\Phi_{G'}}{\partial w_{e_{0}}}({\widetilde{w}}^{\star}) = 0.
```

If this derivative is non-zero, the equilibrium condition fails in the expanded domain, and a new stationary point must be computed in the higher-dimensional space.

### 

### Relation to Variance Expansion

Edge addition introduces a new coordinate direction in the variational domain. This alters both:

As shown in Section 1.7.6, curvature anisotropy induces unequal incident weights; by Section 1.5, such asymmetry generates first-order variance expansion; and by Section 1.6, positive dispersion persists under small perturbations.
